Geospatial Analysis: Vancouver Neighborhood Markets

Market viability assessment by neighborhood | Data: Statistics Canada 2021, Vancouver Open Data

About This Analysis

I built this analysis to explore how location characteristics drive commercial viability in urban neighborhoods. The question I wanted to answer: what makes some neighborhoods more attractive for commercial activity than others? And more importantly—how much does transit accessibility actually matter?

Having lived in English Bay, I've seen firsthand how a neighborhood with good density, walkability, and spending power can sustain a thriving retail scene. But I also noticed wealthier neighborhoods like Shaughnessy doing well commercially despite limited transit access. That observation shaped how I approached the weighting.

Methodology

I scored each neighborhood on three factors:

Income/Spending Power (45%)

This is the primary driver. Retailers and service providers need customers with discretionary income. A neighborhood can have perfect transit and density, but if people don't have money to spend, commercial activity stalls.

Population Density (40%)

Critical mass matters. You need enough people in close proximity to support local retail and services. Density enables walkability, which supports the ecosystem.

Transit Accessibility (15%)

Transit is important, but it's not determinative. I weighted it lower because affluent neighborhoods like Shaughnessy and Dunbar-Southlands clearly sustain strong retail without high-frequency transit. People drive.

Formula: (Income × 0.45) + (Density × 0.40) + (Transit × 0.15) = Viability Score (0-100)

Key Insight

The conventional wisdom in urban planning is that transit drives everything. But Vancouver's neighborhoods tell a different story. Shaughnessy (0/100 transit) outscores Hastings-Sunrise (43/100 transit). Why? Income and density matter more to actual retail viability than planners often admit.

What The Numbers Reveal

High Opportunity (65+)

Downtown (66.8) and Kitsilano (65.9) are the proven markets. Downtown anchored by massive office infrastructure. Kitsilano nearly matches it despite less office space—high income, strong density, good transit create genuine retail viability. English Bay territory.

Medium Opportunity (50-65)

Mount Pleasant, Commercial Drive, Dunbar-Southlands, and Oakridge. Mount Pleasant is dense and gentrifying—rising income + high population = growth trajectory. Dunbar-Southlands and Oakridge represent affluent, car-oriented neighborhoods with strong service/retail markets.

Emerging Markets (<50)

Strathcona, Hastings-Sunrise, Shaughnessy, and others have potential, but the mix of factors doesn't quite align yet. Interesting how Shaughnessy (highest income in the city) is limited by low density and no transit.

What This Means

  • Downtown & Kitsilano: Proven, low-risk markets. Established customer base.
  • Mount Pleasant & Commercial Drive: Growth plays. Gentrifying, rising income, high density, less saturated.
  • Affluent Periphery: Underrated. High-income customers spend money. Luxury retail, professional services thrive.
  • East Side: Early-stage. Smart for niche or long-term strategies.

Closing Thought

Location isn't just about what's there—it's about who's there, how many of them there are, and whether they can access it. Vancouver's neighborhoods show these factors clearly, and the data backs up what you see if you actually walk around the city.
