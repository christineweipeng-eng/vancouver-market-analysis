GEOSPATIAL ANALYSIS: VANCOUVER NEIGHBORHOOD MARKETS
Market Viability Assessment

================================================================

ABOUT THIS ANALYSIS

I built this analysis to explore how location characteristics drive commercial viability in urban neighborhoods. The question I wanted to answer: what makes some neighborhoods more attractive for commercial activity than others? And more importantly—how much does transit accessibility actually matter?

Having lived in English Bay, I've seen firsthand how a neighborhood with good density, walkability, and spending power can sustain a thriving retail scene. But I also noticed wealthier neighborhoods like Shaughnessy doing well commercially despite limited transit access. That observation shaped how I approached the weighting.


METHODOLOGY

I scored each neighborhood on three factors:

Income/Spending Power (45%)
This is the primary driver. Retailers and service providers need customers with discretionary income. A neighborhood can have perfect transit and density, but if people don't have money to spend, commercial activity stalls. This is weighted highest.

Population Density (40%)
Critical mass matters. You need enough people in close proximity to support local retail and services. Density enables walkability, which supports the ecosystem.

Transit Accessibility (15%)
Transit is important, but it's not determinative. I weighted it lower because affluent neighborhoods like Shaughnessy and Dunbar-Southlands clearly sustain strong retail without high-frequency transit. People drive. The market works differently than conventional urban planning theory suggests.

Formula: (Income × 0.45) + (Density × 0.40) + (Transit × 0.15) = Viability Score (0-100)

Data sources: Statistics Canada 2021 Census, Vancouver Open Data Portal, and general market knowledge.

Note: I intentionally omitted existing commercial presence from the score. A neighborhood with few commercial tenants isn't necessarily non-viable—it's just underdeveloped. Market maturity and potential are different things.


WHAT THE NUMBERS REVEAL

High Opportunity (65+): Downtown, Kitsilano

Downtown scores 66.8, anchored by massive existing office infrastructure and exceptional transit. But interestingly, Kitsilano (65.9) nearly matches it—despite having way less office space. Why? High income ($78k median), strong density, and good transit combine to create a genuinely viable retail market. English Bay/West End territory, basically.

Medium Opportunity (50-65): Mount Pleasant, Commercial Drive, Dunbar-Southlands, Oakridge, Sunset, Renfrew-Collingwood

This tier is where I find the most interesting opportunities. Mount Pleasant (59.7) is dense and gentrifying—rising income + high population = growth trajectory. Same with Commercial Drive. Dunbar-Southlands and Oakridge score differently (high income, lower density) but represent another viable model: affluent, car-oriented neighborhoods with strong service/retail markets.

Emerging Markets (<50): Strathcona, Hastings-Sunrise, Shaughnessy, and others

These neighborhoods have potential, but the mix of factors doesn't quite align yet. Shaughnessy is interesting—highest income in the city, but low density and no transit. It still supports retail, but the market operates differently (destination-based, car-dependent).


KEY INSIGHT: INCOME MATTERS MORE THAN TRANSIT

The conventional wisdom in urban planning is that transit drives everything. But Vancouver's neighborhoods tell a different story. Shaughnessy (0/100 transit) outscores Hastings-Sunrise (43/100 transit). Why? Income and density matter more to actual retail viability than planners often admit.

This changes how you think about market opportunity. It's not just "build transit, retail will follow." It's "understand local spending power and population concentration."


WHAT THIS MEANS PRACTICALLY

If I were advising a real estate investor or retailer:

1. Downtown and Kitsilano are proven markets. Low risk. Established customer base, proven commercial infrastructure.

2. Mount Pleasant and Commercial Drive are growth plays. Neighborhoods gentrifying, income rising, density high. Less saturated than downtown. Room to grow.

3. The affluent periphery (Shaughnessy, Dunbar-Southlands) is underrated. Yes, transit is low. But high-income customers spend money. Luxury retail, professional services, destination businesses thrive here.

4. Most of the east side and emerging neighborhoods are early-stage. Not necessarily bad—just less immediately obvious as commercial plays. Could be smart for niche or long-term strategies.


LIMITATIONS & NEXT STEPS

This analysis uses approximate data and a simplified model. Real commercial viability also depends on:
- Actual retail saturation (are there already 10 coffee shops on the block?)
- Specific street-level characteristics (is this corner a destination?)
- Commercial lease rates and availability
- Regulatory environment (zoning, business licenses, etc.)

To build a production version, I'd integrate:
- Real census data via Statistics Canada API
- Actual transit frequency and accessibility metrics
- Commercial real estate databases (lease rates, vacancy)
- Street-level foot traffic data
- Temporal trends (how neighborhoods are changing)

But the core methodology—weighting income, density, and transit by actual commercial viability—would remain the same.


CLOSING THOUGHT

This was a useful exercise in thinking spatially about economic activity. Location isn't just about what's there—it's about who's there, how many of them there are, and whether they can access it. Vancouver's neighborhoods show these factors clearly, and the data backs up what you see if you actually walk around the city.
